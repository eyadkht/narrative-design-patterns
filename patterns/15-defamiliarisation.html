Pattern 15 Framing

Defamiliarisation

Take something the reader knows by heart, and re-draw it strangely. The familiar object becomes a question.

Why it works

Familiar shapes invite skimming. The eye recognises the chart from a thousand others and moves on. Defamiliarisation pulls the rug, the same data in a strange encoding forces a second look, and the assumptions that were running silently get noticed.

How to use it

Pick something the reader knows so well they don't really see it any more, the keyboard, a clock face, the world map. Keep the data the same. Change only one assumption (order, orientation, projection). Don't apologise for the strangeness, that's what's doing the work. Below: QWERTY, then re-sorted by what English actually uses.

Live demo

This is the keyboard under your fingers.

The same 26 letters in two different layouts. Each letter is coloured by how often English actually uses it, the warmer the key, the more you press it. Hit the button and watch what "QWERTY" was hiding.

QWERTY, the layout under your fingers right now

Letter frequencies: Norvig's analysis of the English corpus (~3.5 trillion letters). QWERTY was patented by Christopher Sholes in 1878, its non-ergonomic layout was designed to slow typists down, so that adjacent typewriter levers wouldn't jam.

Real-world example

The upside-down Hobo-Dyer world map shows the same coastlines, only which way is "up" has changed. The keyboard demo above does the same trick on a smaller subject: identical 26 letters, only the ordering removed. August Dvorak's Dvorak Simplified Keyboard (1936) is the real-world version, every QWERTY user feels its strangeness on first sight, and that strangeness is what makes their everyday layout suddenly visible.
