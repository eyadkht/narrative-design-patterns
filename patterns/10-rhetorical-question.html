Pattern 10 Framing Argumentation

Rhetorical Question

Open with a question the reader is already half-asking, then let the chart deliver the answer.

Why it works

A headline that asserts puts the reader on the defensive. A headline that asks puts them in the seat next to you, hunting for the answer in the same data. When the chart resolves the question, the reader credits themselves with the insight, not you.

How to use it

Lead with a punchy rhetorical question as the title. Hold the chart back until the reader opts in (a button, a scroll trigger). Let the data animate in so the answer arrives as discovery, not lecture. Avoid leading questions where the answer is obvious from the framing.

Live demo

Have UK house prices really raced ahead of wages?

Everyone says yes. Most people guess the gap is maybe 2×, prices have grown twice as fast as wages. The reality is sharper than that.

ONS · England & Wales · 1997–2016

Median house price growth ran almost 4× faster than median wage growth over the period, pushing the price-to-earnings ratio from 3.6× to 7.6×.

Real-world example

The ONS housing-affordability bulletin Housing affordability in England and Wales, 1997–2016 opens with the same question we use here. The chart that follows shows house prices rising 259% while wages rose 68%, the question reframes a familiar grumble as a specific, quantified gap.
