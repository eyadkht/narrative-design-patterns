Pattern 14 Framing

Convention Breaking

Set up a chart convention readers expect, then break it, and force them to look twice.

Why it works

Conventions let readers skim, the eye fills in what it expects, and your message slides off. Break a convention (independent axes become shared, a scale flips, a category doesn't belong) and the skim stops. The second look is where the message lands.

How to use it

Show the conventional chart first, then redraw the same data with the convention broken. Don't explain the trick, the contrast does the work. Below: women's representation in two roles, first on independent y-axes, then on a shared one.

Live demo

Two charts. Same data. What changed?

Both bars below show the share of women in a different role within US Fortune 500 companies. The convention is that each chart picks its own y-axis. Flip the toggle to see what that hides.

Sources: US BLS Labor Force Statistics 2023 (labor force share); Fortune 500 list, 2024 (women CEOs).

Real-world example

Reuters' The Glass Ceiling Persists drew women's CEO numbers and their share of the broader workforce on two different y-axes, then redrew them on the same one. On the shared scale, the CEO bar collapses into a sliver, and the chart's argument finally lands.
