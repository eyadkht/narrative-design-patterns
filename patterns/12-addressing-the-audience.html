Pattern 12 Emotion Framing Engagement

Addressing the Audience

Stop talking about people. Talk to you, the reader sitting on the other side of the chart.

Why it works

Third-person copy keeps the reader at a distance: the average person, users, adults aged 25–44. It tells them the data is about other people. Switch to you and the reader becomes the subject, the same number that bounced off them now lands.

How to use it

Frame the headline as a question. Write annotations in second person. Swap the average person for you, users for your. The demo below tries it as a short quiz about your own phone habits.

Live demo

How well do you know your own phone?

Three quick guesses. After each one, the chart will tell you how close you were, and it'll keep talking to you, not about the average person.

    Real-world example

    How Much Do You Know About Milk? is an infographic whose title is a "you" question to the reader. The Pudding's How Common is Your Birthday? uses the same shape: population-wide numbers, but every sentence is addressed to the one person reading.
