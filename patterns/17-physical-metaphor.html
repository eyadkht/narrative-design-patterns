Pattern 17 Framing

Physical Metaphor

Direction carries feeling. Up = good, down = bad. Real data, drawn as a thing that climbs or falls, gets understood before the labels are even read.

Why it works

We learn up = good before we learn to read. Salaries "rise". Careers "climb". You "fall behind". When a chart matches that wiring, the meaning arrives before the labels do, the shape says raise or cut before the dollar figure does.

How to use it

Pick the spatial direction before the chart type. Good news climbs, bad news falls. Below, US median earnings by education sit as rungs on a ladder. Climb up to feel a raise. Step down to feel a cut.

Live demo

Each rung is a real median salary. Move up or down.

Eight US education levels, sorted by median pay. The bars on the right show what share of US adults end up at each rung, most sit in the middle. Climb up from “high school diploma” (where 28% of adults stop) and feel the raise; step down and feel the cut.

You start with a high-school diploma: median pay $46,020.

Climb or descend. The chart didn't say "up is better" anywhere, but you already know it is.

Salary — BLS, “Education pays” 2024 (median weekly earnings × 52, full-time workers 25+). Share — US Census Bureau, CPS/ACS 2023 educational attainment, adults 25+. Some-college / Associate split derived from 2018 Census proportions (2023 publishes them combined). Figures rounded to nearest $100 / 0.1%.

Real-world example

The FT's A drop in China's demand could drag these countries down uses the same wiring: country bubbles literally fall on the page when China's imports drop. No y-axis labels needed, the falling motion is the bad news.
